Y is a connection game (Schensted–Titus, 1953) played on a triangular board of
side *n* — *n* rows, *n*(*n*+1)/2 cells. Each move places one stone; you win by
connecting **all three sides** of the triangle with a single group. Because a
filled board always has exactly one winner, **Y can never draw**, and a
strategy-stealing argument shows the **first player wins at every size**. Hex
reduces to Y, which is what makes Y worth studying in its own right.

This project strongly solved Y from side 1 through side 6 with its open-source
[retrograde engine](https://github.com/brianhliou/solved-games/tree/main/engine) —
computing the game-theoretic value of *every* position, not just the opening. The
explorer below plays perfect moves for sides 3–5, solving the whole board live in
your browser.

## The family, by size

| Side | Cells | Positions | Result |
|-----:|------:|----------:|--------|
| 1 | 1 | 2 | First player wins |
| 2 | 3 | 13 | First player wins |
| 3 | 6 | 257 | First player wins |
| 4 | 10 | 16,505 | First player wins |
| 5 | 15 | 3,337,584 | First player wins |
| 6 | 21 | 2,173,243,128 † | First player wins |

All sides above are **strongly** solved by this project. Y is a first-player win
at every size, with no draws.

*† Sides 1–5 count reachable positions; side 6 is the dense-index space
(reachable positions were not separately enumerated). The explorer solves over
the dense index, so its side-5 count (3,492,117) is slightly higher than the
reachable count shown here.*
